The Blockchain and Fintech, an Overview

June 06, 2016

Reading time ~3 minutes

From Bitcoin to the Blockchain

At the end of 2008, a thesis was posted by a person named Satoshi Nakamoto on a US mailing list where cryptographers exchange information. This was the very beginning of Bitcoin, a digital currency with at its heart the first implementation of a blockchain. With the banking crisis in 2008, Bitcoin came at a right time. Techies, libertarians and open source advocates saw Bitcoin as the new and better money, not managed by any central authority, easy and fast and global. This utopian dream fueled a hype, but at the time, Bitcoin was not yet taken seriously.

When later, in 2013, the cryptocurrency peaked at over USD 1000, it caught a lot of attention in the mainstream media. I think it was at around this time; 2013-2014, that the Bitcoin hype started to attract the attention of banks as well:

  • The highly volatile price of bitcoin got investors interested
  • People started to experiment with alternate blockchains, creating their own cryptocurrencies
  • Trading platforms for altcoins (alternate cryptocurrencies) started to emerge online
  • Banks saw Bitcoin as a potential threat to traditional money

After a couple of security breaches and scandals such as the collapse of the largest exchange Mt. Gox in 2014, Bitcoin’s value fell down to $200. But by this time, the cat was out of the bag. Some entrepreneurs and the first banks had looked at the core concepts of the cryptocurrency and started to realize the potential of the underlying technology of Bitcoin; The blockchain.

The blockchain is seen as the main technological innovation of Bitcoin, since it stands as proof of all the transactions on the network. A block is the ‘current’ part of a blockchain which records some or all of the recent transactions, and once completed goes into the blockchain as permanent database. Each time a block gets completed, a new block is generated. There is a countless number of such blocks in the blockchain. So are the blocks randomly placed in a blockchain? No, they are linked to each other (like a chain) in proper linear, chronological order with every block containing a hash of the previous block. ~Investopedia

Bitcoin could end up being the MySpace of cryptocurrency, but the underlying technology powering new payment methods is here to stay. ~ Brock Pierce, dec 2014

Banks and stock exchanges and financial institutions started block chain technology businesses better, faster, cheaper, and more efficient but you the consumer will never even realise you’re using it in many instances,

To use conventional banking as an analogy, the blockchain is like a full history of banking transactions. Bitcoin transactions are entered chronologically in a blockchain just the way bank transactions are. Blocks, meanwhile, are like individual bank statements.

Based on the Bitcoin protocol, the blockchain database is shared by all nodes participating in a system. The full copy of the blockchain has records of every Bitcoin transaction ever executed. It can thus provide insight about facts like how much value belonged a particular address at any point in the past.

The ever-growing size of the blockchain is considered by some to be a problem due to issues like storage and synchronization. On an average, every 10 minutes, a new block is appended to the block chain through mining.
